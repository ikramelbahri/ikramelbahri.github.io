Credit Card Default Prediction: When Accuracy Lies

A model that got 81% of predictions right, and still would have been a bad decision to actually use. This is the story of why — and what a genuinely useful credit risk model looks like once you account for it.

ToolsPython, scikit-learn, pandas
Data sourceUCI Default of Credit Card Clients (30,000 accounts)
MethodLogistic Regression & Random Forest

The set-up

The dataset is 30,000 real credit card clients in Taiwan, tracked over six months in 2005: what they owed each month, what they paid, and how late they were. The task is simple to state — predict who defaults the following month — but the way most people evaluate a model like this (accuracy) turns out to hide the one thing that actually matters to a lender.

About 78% of clients in this dataset never default. That single fact quietly shapes everything that follows.

The accuracy trap

The first model I built, a plain Logistic Regression, scored 81% accuracy. On paper, that looks like a strong result. But accuracy just measures "how often was the model right overall" — and when 78% of people never default, a model can get most of its calls right without doing anything useful at all, simply by leaning toward "safe" most of the time.

Looking past the headline number told a different story:

For a lender, a missed defaulter is the expensive kind of mistake — credit gets extended to someone who won't pay it back. A model that misses seven out of every ten actual defaulters isn't a strong model wearing a good number; it's a weak model wearing a flattering one.

Three ways to fix it — and what each one actually does

The imbalance is the root problem, so the fix has to address that directly, not just tweak the model at the edges. I tried three approaches, each working on a different part of the pipeline:

1. Lower the decision threshold

By default, a model calls something "default" only if it's more than 50% confident. Lowering that bar to 30% catches more real defaulters, at the cost of more false alarms. This is a genuinely useful lever — but it's worth being precise about what it does and doesn't change. It doesn't make the model smarter. It just moves where you draw the line on the exact same underlying predictions. I confirmed this directly: the plain model and the threshold-adjusted model share an identical ROC-AUC of 0.744 — proof that nothing about the model's actual ability to separate defaulters from non-defaulters had changed, only where I chose to act on it.

2. Reweight the training itself

class_weight='balanced' tells the model, during training, to treat a mistake on a defaulter as roughly 3.5x more costly than a mistake on a non-defaulter — roughly matching how outnumbered defaulters actually are in the data. Unlike the threshold trick, this genuinely changes what the model learns, not just how its output gets read. Recall on defaulters jumped from 30% to 60%.

3. Try a different kind of model

Random Forest can capture non-linear patterns — the idea that risk might not rise smoothly with lateness, but jump sharply past some threshold. With the same class balancing applied, it edged out Logistic Regression on every statistical measure: recall of 61%, and a meaningfully higher ROC-AUC of 0.777 against 0.747.

On pure statistics, Random Forest is the clear winner. But statistics aren't the whole story here — see the cost analysis below for why that conclusion doesn't survive contact with real money.

What actually predicts default: a real example

Rather than feed the model 18 raw monthly columns, I engineered features that capture trajectory — is someone's debt growing or shrinking, are they paying less of their bill over time, how many months in a row were they late. Look only at April, and this person looks perfectly safe — zero balance, nothing owed. Look at the full trajectory, and a different picture emerges: someone who wasn't using their card at all, then suddenly started spending, immediately fell behind, made one partial catch-up payment, then stopped paying while their balance kept climbing. A snapshot model would have missed this person entirely. A trajectory-aware model, using features like bill_trend (their debt grew by NT$3,913 over the window) and months_delayed, would flag them correctly.

This wasn't just a hunch that paid off — it's borne out directly in the Random Forest's feature importance. The top four features — most recent lateness, months delayed, and two engineered trend measures — account for roughly half the model's total predictive weight. Three of those four didn't exist in the original file; they were built. Meanwhile, age, sex, education, and marital status don't appear in the top fifteen features at all. Behavior predicts default. Who someone is, on this data, barely does.

Turning statistics into money

ROC-AUC and recall are useful, but they don't answer the question a lender actually has: which model saves the most money? So I translated every model's mistakes into an estimated NT$ cost — a missed defaulter costs roughly what that person owed (using their real average bill from the data, not a guessed number), and a false alarm costs a smaller, assumed fraction of that bill in lost interest revenue.

Model Comparison

Estimated real financial cost of each approach, in NT$.

The naive, unadjusted model — the one with the "best" 81% accuracy — turned out to be the most expensive by far, at an estimated NT$40.9M in losses. Every version that addressed the imbalance problem cost meaningfully less, saving up to NT$15.25M (37%).

Here's the more interesting twist: Random Forest had the best ROC-AUC of every model tested, but balanced Logistic Regression was actually the cheaper option in NT$ terms — NT$25.7M versus NT$26.2M. Random Forest catches slightly more defaulters, but it also raises more false alarms, and at a conservative cost assumption, those false alarms add up to more than the extra defaulters it catches are worth.

I didn't want to rest that conclusion on one arbitrary cost assumption, so I re-ran the comparison across a range of false-positive costs, from 5% to 30% of a client's bill:

  • At low false-positive costs, balanced Logistic Regression wins
  • As false-positive costs rise, Random Forest overtakes it — fewer false alarms start to matter more
  • The naive, unadjusted model never becomes competitive at any assumption tested

The honest conclusion isn't "Model X wins." It's that addressing class imbalance at all is unambiguously worth it, and the choice between the remaining good options depends on a real number — what a false alarm actually costs the business — that only the lender itself can supply.

Data notes

Two data quality issues were worth catching rather than assuming away. EDUCATION and MARRIAGE contained category codes (0, 5, 6 in EDUCATION; 0 in MARRIAGE) that don't appear anywhere in the official documentation — folded into the existing "Other" category rather than dropped, to avoid losing data over an edge case. More significantly, the six PAY_ status columns contain two undocumented values (0 and -2) that together make up over half the dataset. They couldn't be dropped or ignored at that scale, so they were kept as-is and reported honestly as an interpretive ambiguity in the source data, rather than given an invented, confident meaning.

What this doesn't prove

2005 Taiwanese data, not current UK data

This project demonstrates a methodology — imbalanced classification, cost-sensitive evaluation, behavioral feature engineering — rather than making a claim about current UK consumer credit risk specifically.

The cost assumptions are estimates, not facts

False-positive cost was tested across a 5–30% range as a reasonable estimate. A real deployment would need actual figures from the business, not an analyst's assumption.

A single train/test split

Given the dataset's size this is a defensible choice, but cross-validation would give a more robust estimate of how stable these results really are.
